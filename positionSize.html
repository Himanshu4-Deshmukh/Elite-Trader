Position Sizing


When trading, position sizing is an important risk management strategy. In fact, before you start a trade, position sizing is the first step in risk management.

It is a method for determining the size of a trader's position during trading, as the name suggests. To put it another way, it means how much money a trader invests in a particular trade.


When determining the size of a trading position, a trader's risk tolerance as well as his trading capital or portfolio should be taken into consideration.

When deciding how much of a security to buy or sell, professional traders use the concept of position sizing. They can benefit from this by minimizing risk and maximizing the returns.


You may end up taking too much risk or not enough risk to make a profit from a trade if the size of your position is too large or too small.





How to calculate Position Size ?


Let's first try to understand two important aspects of the ideal position size's calculation before figuring out how to do it:


  • Account Risk
  • Trade Risk


    • The term "Account Risk" refers to a predetermined amount or percentage of your trading capital that serves as a limit on the amount of risk you are willing to take on each trade.


    So, for example if your trading capital is of 1,00,000 and you have set a percentage risk limit at 1% then you are willing to take risk upto 1000 per trade.

    For most trades, a trader should not risk more than 2% to 5% of his or her trading capital. In addition, the account risk limit should remain the same for all trades.


    The difference between the point at which you place your stop-loss order and the trade entry point is referred to as trade risk.


    So, for instance, your trade risk is $10 per share if you buy shares of a company for $100 and set a stop loss at $90.

    You can now determine the ideal size of your trade's position once you know how far the stop loss is from your entry point.


    Ideal Position Size = Account Risk / Trade Risk




  • Example -

    • Consider a trader with $5,000,000 in trading capital who wants to go long on shares of ABC Company at $80 with a $76 stop loss and a $90 price target.


    Position Sizing Now the ideal trading size for the trader can be calculated as follows -


    Assuming 2% risk limit set by the trader for all trades, the account risk in this case will be 2% of 5,00,000 = $10,000


    Trade Risk = Entry - Stop Loss = 80 - 76 = $4


    Now ideal Position Size = 10,000/ 4 = 2500 Shares.


    Let's say ABC Company trades derivatives with a 500-share trading lot.

    The person now has the option of trading 5 lots (2500/500) in derivatives or trading 2500 shares in the cash market.


    No matter what kind of strategy you're using, how the trade is set up, or the market conditions are, if you follow this method, you'll get the right size for your position.

    Keep in mind that a significant trading position always causes anxiety.


    Jack Schwager, a well-known American author and trader, quotes,


    "The greater the position, the greater the risk that trading decisions will be influenced by fear rather than experience and judgment."


    Therefore, you should always trade within your emotional and financial capabilities. Position sizing is an important factor in this situation.
