Candlestick charts were originally used by rice traders in Japan in the 17th century. Today, investors and traders from all over the world use them to trade securities.


The first thing you need to know about candlestick charts is how to use them if you want to start trading.


In technical analysis, a candlestick is a type of price chart that shows the opening, high, low, and closing prices of a security for a given time. Such a chart is very informative and visually appealing. It reveals the present and past actions of market participants. As a result, the majority of traders worldwide use it.


Candlestick patterns are groups of two or more candles arranged in a particular order that form patterns. Candlestick patterns are used by traders to identify trading opportunities because they enable them to study the security's past and current price movement to identify trend reversals or trend continuations.


Studying candlestick patterns is crucial because they reveal the actions of buyers and sellers.


Let us first comprehend the fundamentals of candle formation before moving on.


Candles can be of two type - Bullish & Bearish



1. Bullish Candle

A bullish candle is one in which the closing price is higher than the opening price. The color white or green fills the center of such a candle.

such a candle signifies positive sentiment and indicate that the price of the security is likely to increase further.



2. Bearish Candle

A bearish candle is one in which the closing price is lower than the opening price. Such a candle has a black or red filling in its center. A candle of this kind suggests a negative mood and that the security's price is likely to fall further.



  • Upper Shadow -

    • It is the vertical line between the high of the day and the close (for Bullish Candle) or the open (for Bearish Candle).



  • Body -

    • It's the wide part of a candle, where the difference between the open and closing prices is shown. If the open is lower than the close, the candle is bullish and its real body is white or green; if the close is lower than the open, the candle is bearish and its real body is black or red in this instance.



  • Lower Shadow -

    • It is the vertical line between the low of the day and the open (for bullish Candle) or the close (for Bearish Candle).




    Types of Candlestick Patterns :

    Candlesticks can be classified into three categories based on the number of candles in the patterns.


    • 1. Single Candlestick Patterns
    • 2. Two Candlestick Patterns
    • 3. Three Candlestick Patterns



    1. Single Candlestick Patterns -


    These patterns are created by a single candle, as the name suggests.


    Following are Popular single Candlestick patterns -

  • Bullish Marubozu
  • Bearish Marubozu
  • Hammer
  • Hanging Man
  • Inverted Hammer
  • Shooting Star
  • Doji


    • i. Bullish Marubozu pattern -


    In technical analysis, bullish marubozu is a single candlestick pattern used to predict bullishness. The Japanese word Marubozu means "Bald." Bullish Marubozu is essentially a long, white or green candle with no upper or lower shadows at all.



    It occurs when the low and high are nearly equal to the open and close, respectively. It is thought to be a very strong indication of bullishness.



    ii. Bearish Marubozu Pattern -


    Technical analysis uses the bearish Marubozu pattern, which is a single candlestick pattern, to predict bearishness.


    Bearish Marubozu is essentially a long, black or red bearish candle with no or very little upper or lower shadow.

    It occurs when the high and low are nearly equal to the open and close, respectively. It is regarded as a particularly potent signal of bearishness.



    iii. Hammer Pattern -


    At the bottom of a downtrend, a candle with a small body, little or no upper shadow, and a long lower shadow creates this candlestick pattern. It is a candlestick pattern of a bullish reversal.


    In most of the cases, the lower shadow should be at least twice of the real body. The color of the real body can be red or green. But the stronger bullish signal comes from the hammer formed with the green real body.


    This pattern act as a warning signal for a potential upside reversal.




    iv. Hanging Man Pattern -


    A candle with a small body, little or no upper shadow, and a long lower shadow at the top of an uptrend creates this candlestick pattern. It is a candlestick pattern of a bearish reversal.


    In most of the cases, the lower shadow should be at least twice of the real body. The color of the real body can be red or green. But the stronger Bearish signal comes from the Hanging man formed with the Red real body.


    This pattern act as a warning signal for a potential downside reversal.




    v. Inverted Hammer Pattern -


    This pattern looks opposite of the hammer pattern. The pattern is a bullish reversal candlestick.


    It is created by a candle at the bottom of a downtrend that has a long upper shadow, a small body, and little or no lower shadow.


    The upper shadow should typically be at least twice as big as the actual body in this pattern. The actual body may be green or red in color. However, a stronger bullish signal is provided by the inverted hammer formed by the green real body.


    This pattern suggests that buyers are driving up the price, reversing the downward trend.




    vi. Shooting Star Pattern -


    This pattern looks opposite of the hanging man pattern. It is a candlestick pattern of a bearish reversal. It is created by a candle at the top of an uptrend that has a small body, little or no lower shadow, and a long upper shadow.


    The upper shadow should typically be at least twice as big as the actual body in this pattern. The actual body may be green or red in color. However, a shooting star with a red body is more of a bearish sign.

    This pattern indicates that sellers are driving the price lower while buyers are losing ground, indicating a trend reversal to the downside.




    vii. Doji Pattern -


    When the closing price and the opening price are nearly the same, a doji candlestick pattern is formed. The upper and lower shadows' lengths can vary. Doji denotes market indecisiveness because buyers and sellers are equal and cause price movement confusion.

    Many traders believe that doji is an important price reversal indicator as there is uncertainty in price movement. To understand what this candlestick says, prior price action building up to the doji is carefully observed.


    For example, it may take the price lower if it is formed at the top of an uptrend and may take the price higher if it is formed at the bottom of a downtrend.


    Doji is a warning sign of reversal because it represents equality or indecision between the bulls and the bears.


    It is thought to be a stronger signal when Doji is coupled with volume dry up. Doji patterns are divided into four categories based on the length of the shadow: standard doji, long-legged doji, dragonfly doji, and gravestone doji.


    Standard Doji -

    It is the very basic doji candlestick pattern which can occur at either the top or bottom of a trend to signal a trend reversal. Its shape is similar to 'cross' or 'plus sign'.


    Long Legged Doji -

    The upper and lower shadows of this candlestick pattern are lengthy.


    Dragonfly Doji -

    This candlestick pattern has no or very little upper shadow and a long lower shadow. It resembles the English letter "T" in shape. It's a bullish reversal candlestick pattern that suggests an asset's price will likely rise further.


    Gravestone Doji -

    This pattern is opposite to dragonfly doji, this pattern has no or very little lower shadow and a long upper shadow. This is a bearish reversal candlestick pattern that suggests an asset's price is likely to fall further.
