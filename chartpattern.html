A chart pattern is an integral aspect of Technical Analysis. It is a visual representation of price movement of a security which is formed using past and current price of an asset. It helps to predict what the price of securities might do in the future and represents the collective behavior of human nature.


In layman's terms, chart patterns help us determine who will win the price battle between bulls and bears.


Traders who are able to read charts and use that knowledge in their decisions about when to buy and sell increase their chances of being on the right side of the trend. Thus in the event that you are beginning your exchanging venture, you must figure out how to recognize and interpret Chart Patterns.


Chart patterns are "fractal in nature," which means that they can be applied to any time period, including daily, weekly, monthly, 15 minutes, 30 minutes, and so on.


The first step in becoming a successful trader is to train your eyes to recognize chart patterns by studying any security's price chart. Regular practice is required for this. Make it a daily routine to examine the charts of various securities.






Types of Chart Patterns :-


Reversal and continuation chart patterns are the two main types of chart patterns.


Reversal patterns are chart patterns that occur when price action indicates a change in the trend's direction.


Simply put, a reversal pattern during an uptrend indicates that the price is likely to fall, whereas a reversal pattern during a downtrend indicates that the price is more likely to rise.


Similarly, chart patterns are referred to as "Continuation Patterns" when price action indicates that the trend will continue.


Simply put, if a continuation pattern appears during an uptrend, it indicates that the price is more likely to rise, whereas if it appears during a downtrend, it indicates that the price is more likely to fall.


There are also a few neutral patterns, which can either indicate a reversal or a continuation of the previous trend based on the breakout direction.


There are a lot of different chart patterns, but as traders, our job is to know the ones that are often seen on charts and give highly accurate results. The most significant Chart Patterns, which can be applied to the majority of financial markets, are outlined below.





Reversal Chart Patterns :-

    • i. Head and Shoulder -


    It is a well-known trend reversal pattern that is easy to spot and one of the most reliable.


    After an established uptrend, it is a bearish reversal pattern that indicates a significant reversal upon completion. The price is likely to fall from this point forward as this pattern indicates that buyers have lost the battle.


    This pattern has three peaks, as shown in the chart below. The left shoulder is the first peak, the head is the second peak (the highest), and the right shoulder is the third peak. The line framed by interfacing the lower part of the left and right shoulder is known as the neck area. When the price breaks the neckline on the downside, the pattern is said to be complete.

    After a long bullish trend, the price rises to a peak and then falls to form a trough, as shown in the chart above. The price then declines once more before reaching a second high that is significantly higher than the initial peak. For the third time, the price goes up, but only to the first peak before falling back to the neckline.


    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the downside, a short entry is taken.


    The potential target for the pattern is normally calculated below the neckline equal to the price difference between the high point of the head and the neckline.

    Stop loss will be placed just above the previous high as shown in the figure.
    When the price breaks below the neckline, a significant rise in volume is considered to be more reliable for the pattern.



    ii. Inverted Head and Shoulder -


    As the name indicates, it is an inverted version of the head and shoulder pattern explained above.


    After a clearly defined downtrend, it is a bullish reversal pattern that indicates a significant reversal upon completion.


    The price is likely to rise from hereon out, as this pattern indicates that sellers have lost the battle.

    This pattern is comprised of three lows, as depicted in the chart below. The first low is referred to as the left shoulder, the second low—the lowest one—is referred to as the head, and the third low is referred to as the right shoulder. The neckline is the line that forms when the tops of the left and right shoulders meet. When the price breaks the neckline on the upside, the pattern is said to be complete.

    The price then rises to form a peak after a prolonged bearish trend, as depicted in the chart above. The price rises once more before falling again to a second low that is significantly lower than the first low. For the third time, the price drops to the level of the first low before rising again to the neckline.


    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the upside, a long entry is made.


    Typically, the price difference between the low point of the head and the neckline serves as the pattern's potential target above the neckline.


    As depicted in the figure above, the stop loss will be placed at the left shoulder's edge. When the price breaks above the neckline, it is thought that the pattern is more reliable if there is a significant increase in volume.



    iii. Double Top -


    After an established uptrend, it is a bearish reversal pattern that indicates a significant reversal upon completion.


    On charts, this pattern is easy to spot. It resembles the letter "M" in English. The price is likely to fall from this point forward as this pattern indicates that buyers have lost the battle.


    As seen in the chart below, this pattern is formed by two consecutive tops and a moderate decline between the two tops. The horizontal line drawn at the bottom formed between the two tops is known as the neckline. The pattern is said to be complete only when the price breaks the neckline on the downside.


    The price rises to form a top and then retraces back to form a bottom after a long bullish trend, as shown in the chart above. The cost rises again to frame a second top which is practically equivalent to the principal top, prior to declining again to the neck area.


    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the downside, a short entry is taken.


    Typically, the price difference between the double top's high point and neckline serves as the pattern's potential target below the neckline.


    We typically observe some consolidation occurring prior to the breakout, which will form a minor swing high and serve as the pattern's stop loss area. We will achieve a favorable risk-to-reward ratio if the stop loss is placed appropriately.


    When the price breaks below the neckline, a significant rise in volume is considered to be more reliable for the pattern.



    iv. Double Bottom -


    It is a bullish reversal pattern that develops after a clear downturn and denotes a significant reversal upon pattern completion.


    On charts, this pattern is easy to spot. It resembles the letter "W" in English. The price is likely to rise from hereon out, as this pattern indicates that sellers have lost the battle.

    This pattern is formed by two consecutive bottoms and a moderate rise between the two bottoms. The horizontal line drawn at the peak formed between the two bottoms is known as the neckline. The pattern is said to be complete only when the price breaks the neckline on the upside.


    The price falls to a bottom after a long bearish trend, then retraces to a degree to a top. The price then rises to the neckline before falling once more to form a second bottom that is nearly the same as the first bottom.


    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the upside, a long entry is made.


    Typically, the price difference between the low point of the double bottom and the neckline serves as the pattern's potential target above the neckline.


    Before the pattern's neckline is breached, consolidation typically occurs before the breakout. This consolidation will form a minor swing low, which will serve as the pattern's stop-loss area.


    When the price breaks above the neckline, it is thought that the pattern is more reliable if there is a significant increase in volume.



    v. Rounding Bottom -


    After a long period of consolidation and a clearly defined downtrend, it is a bullish reversal pattern that indicates a significant reversal upon completion.


    On charts, this pattern is easy to spot. Since its appearance is like the lower part of a bowl or a saucer, the pattern is otherwise called a saucer bottom pattern.


    After a period of consolidation, sellers have lost the battle, as evidenced by this pattern, and prices are likely to rise thereafter.


    This pattern emerges following a downtrend during a period of consolidation, as depicted in the chart above. During this time, the price fall slows down, a bottom forms, and then the price slowly starts to rise.


    The neckline of the rounding bottom pattern is the horizontal line that runs across the top of the bearish and bullish sides. When the price breaks the neckline on the upside, the pattern is said to be complete.


    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the upside, a long entry is made.


    Typically, the price difference between the low point of the rounding bottom and the neckline serves as the pattern's potential target above the neckline.


    The stop loss will be set at the swing low in this pattern, which occurs before the price breaks the neckline on the upside, We will have a good risk-to-reward ratio as a result of this.


    If you observe high volumes during the downtrend, flat or low volumes during the consolidation period, and again increased volume when price breaks above the neckline, the pattern is thought to be more reliable.


    When it forms on weekly or monthly charts, this pattern, which is typically regarded as a long-term pattern, provides a clear signal.



    vi. Rounding Top -


    After a long period of consolidation and a clearly defined uptrend, it is a long-term bearish reversal pattern that indicates a significant reversal upon completion.


    On charts, this pattern is easy to spot. It's also known as an inverted saucer bottom pattern, and it looks exactly like the rounding bottom pattern. After a time of consolidation, buyers have lost the battle, as evidenced by this pattern, and prices are likely to fall from this point forward.


    This pattern is formed during a period of consolidation after an uptrend. During this period the price rise is slowly arrested, a top is formed and then the price slowly starts falling. The neckline of the rounding top pattern is the horizontal line that runs across the bottom of the bearish and bullish sides. When the price breaks the neckline on the downside, the pattern is said to be complete.

    Now, when the price breaks the neckline on the downside, a short entry is taken.


    Typically, the price difference between the rounding top's high point and the neckline serves as the pattern's potential target below the neckline.


    The stop loss will be set at the swing high, which occurs before the price breaks the neckline on the downside, in the event of this pattern. We will have a good risk-to-reward ratio as a result of this.


    If you observe high volumes during the uptrend, flat or low volumes during the consolidation period, and again increased volume when the price breaks below the neckline, the pattern is thought to be more reliable.


    When it forms on weekly or monthly charts, this pattern, which is typically regarded as a long-term pattern, provides a clear signal.
