What is Intermarket Analysis ?


A type of technical analysis called intermarket analysis looks at how different asset classes interact in financial markets. It focuses on identifying trends and potential trading opportunities by analyzing the interactions between currencies, stocks, bonds, commodities, and other financial instruments.


Intermarket analysis is based on the idea that the various asset classes are interconnected and have an impact on one another. Understanding these relationships can help traders comprehend the larger market environment and make better trading decisions. A trader who is aware of the relationship between interest rates and bond prices and currency values, for instance, may be able to use it to anticipate changes in these markets.


Because it can help to identify trends and opportunities that are driven by broader economic factors rather than individual company or asset-specific news, traders who use a macroeconomic approach to trading frequently use intermarket analysis.



  • Example


    • Let's say a trader is interested in trading the S&P 500, which is a stock market index that represents the performance of 500 large-cap stocks in the US. The trader believes that the price of the S&P 500 is influenced by a variety of factors, including interest rates, commodity prices, and currency values.


    To apply intermarket analysis, the trader might start by looking at the US Treasury bond market, as changes in bond yields can affect the overall level of interest rates. If bond yields are rising, it may suggest that interest rates will also rise, which could be negative for stocks as higher rates can reduce borrowing and spending by companies and consumers.

If bond yields are rising, it may suggest that interest rates will also rise, which could be negative for stocks as higher rates can reduce borrowing and spending by companies and consumers.


    Next, the trader might look at the commodity markets, particularly the price of oil, as this can be an important driver of inflation and can also impact the profitability of companies in the energy sector. If oil prices are rising, it may suggest that inflation is also rising, which could lead to higher interest rates and potentially lower stock prices.


    Finally, the trader might consider the currency markets, as changes in currency values can impact the competitiveness of companies that rely on exports or imports. If the US dollar is strengthening against other currencies, it could make US exports more expensive and less competitive, which could be negative for US stocks.


    By analyzing the relationships between these different markets, the trader can gain a broader understanding of the factors that are likely to influence the price of the S&P 500 and make more informed trading decisions.
