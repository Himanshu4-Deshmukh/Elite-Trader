    • 1. Rising Channel


    The Rising Channel Pattern is made up of two parallel, upward-inclining trend lines that serve as support and resistance. The pattern has higher highs and lower lows and occurs during an uptrend.


    If the price breaks up on the upside, it is a continuation pattern, and if it breaks down on the downside, it is a reversal pattern.


    Based on where the breakout is placed, this pattern suggests that buyers and sellers are engaged in a tug-of-war despite the price increase, depending on placement of the breakout.


    In the case of this pattern, traders make their decisions based on where the breakout occurs. For instance, if the price breaks the resistance on the upside, a long trade is made (continuation of the current uptrend), while a short trade is made (reversal of the current uptrend).


    Typically, the height of the channel, or height between the two parallel trend lines above or below the breakout point, is used to project the pattern's potential target, depending on where the breakout is located.


    If the price breaks the resistance on the upside, the stop loss is placed just below the upper resistance, and if the price breaks the support on the downside, the stop loss is placed just above the lower support. The location of the stop loss is determined by the location of the breakout.


    When price breaks support or resistance, a significant rise in volume is considered to be more reliable for the pattern.



    2. Falling Channel -


    The support and resistance of the Falling Channel Pattern are provided by two parallel trend lines that slope downward.


    The pattern is characterized by lower highs and lower lows and occurs during a downtrend.


    If price breaks down, it acts as a continuation pattern and if price breaks up, it acts as a reversal pattern.


    Depending on where the breakout is, this pattern suggests that, despite the falling price, sellers and buyers are engaged in a tug-of-war, with one side ultimately winning.

    In this pattern, traders make their decisions based on where the breakout occurs. For example, if the price breaks the resistance on the upside, a long trade is made (a reversal of the current downtrend), and if the price breaks the support on the downside, a short trade is made (a continuation of the current downtrend).


    Typically, the height of the channel, or height between the two parallel trend lines above or below the breakout point, is used to project the pattern's potential target, depending on where the breakout is located.


    If the price breaks resistance on the upside, the stop loss should be placed just below the upper resistance, and if the price breaks support on the downside, the stop loss should be placed just above the lower support.


    When price breaks support or resistance, a significant rise in volume is considered to be more reliable for the pattern.



    3. Rising Wedge -


    Rising Wedge is a bearish pattern with a wide opening that narrows as the price rises and the trading range narrows.


    The pattern is made up of two lines that converge, an upper resistance line and a lower support line, so the wedge will have an upward slope. The pattern's bullishness is decreasing, as these two lines converge.


    If it occurs during an uptrend, it acts as a reversal pattern, while if it occurs during a downtrend, it acts as a continuation pattern. In both instances, the price appears to be breaking the lower support line on the downside in the rising wedge pattern.


    This pattern indicates that, despite the price rise within the wedge, buyers and sellers are engaged in a tug-of-war, with the sellers ultimately winning.

    4. Falling Wedge -


    A bullish pattern known as the "Falling Wedge" has a wide opening and then narrows as the price drops and the trading range narrows.


    The pattern is made up of two lines that converge, an upper resistance line and a lower support line, so the wedge will have a slope that goes down. The pattern's bearishness is decreasing, as these two lines converge.


    It acts as continuation pattern if it occurs during an uptrend and acts as a reversal pattern if it occurs during a downtrend. In both the cases, in case of falling wedge pattern, the price is seen breaking the upper resistance line on the upside.


    This pattern indicates that, despite the wedge's price fall, sellers and buyers are engaged in a tug-of-war that ultimately results in the buyers winning.

    When the price breaks the upper resistance line on the upside, the pattern is said to be complete. This is when a long entry is taken in the security.


    When the price breaks above the upper resistance line, it is thought that the pattern is more reliable if there is a significant rise in volume.


    Because there is no method for measuring the target in this pattern, it is recommended to use a trailing stop loss or other aspects of technical analysis to forecast price targets.

    5. Symmetrical Triangle -


    The symmetrical triangle pattern is formed by two converging trend lines that are symmetrical in relation to the horizontal line.


    Depending on the previous trend and where the breakout is, the pattern can be either a continuation or a reversal.


    There are at least two higher lows and two lower highs in this pattern, which is characterized by a falling trend line (the "resistance line") and a rising trend line (the "support line") that converge at a point.





    i. Symmetrical Triangle as a Continuation Pattern


    The pattern is referred to as a bullish continuation symmetrical triangle if it is broken to the upside during an uptrend.


    In a similar vein, the pattern is referred to as a bearish continuation symmetrical triangle if it is broken to the downside during a downtrend.






    ii. Symmetrical Triangle as a Reversal Pattern


    The pattern is called a bullish reversal symmetrical triangle if it breaks to the upside during a downtrend. In a similar vein, the pattern is referred to as a bearish reversal symmetrical triangle if it is broken to the downside during an uptrend.


    In the case of the symmetrical triangle pattern, traders make their decisions based on where the breakout occurs. For example, if the price breaks the resistance on the upside, a long trade is made, and if it breaks the support on the downside, a short trade is made.


    The potential target for the pattern is normally calculated projecting the height of the symmetrical triangle at its thickest point above or below the breakout point, depending on placement of the breakout.


    Depending on the placement of the breakout, the stop loss will be placed below or above the breakout zone.

    When a breakout occurs, a significant rise in volume is considered to be more reliable for the pattern.

    Always remember to check the volume activity to see if the breakout is real or fake. Increased volume at the breakout is always associated with a real breakout.
